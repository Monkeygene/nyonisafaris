Birding at Lake Malawi

by Eugene Booysen

In this blog post, you'll uncover the wonders of birding at Lake Malawi, one of Africa's most captivating freshwater lakes. Situated within the scenic landscapes of southeastern Africa, Lake Malawi (also known as Lake Nyasa) is a birdwatcher's paradise, offering diverse habitats and abundant birdlife. Discover its vibrant ecology, unique bird species, lush islands, and why Lake Malawi National Park is globally recognized by UNESCO for its conservation significance.

Quiet beach walk on the shores of Kachere, Lake Malawi

Birdwatching at Lake Malawi's Rich Habitats and Islands

Nestled along the Great Rift Valley, Lake Malawi spans the borders of Malawi, Tanzania, and Mozambique. Predominantly under Malawi's territory, this stunning lake measures about 580 kilometers long and 75 kilometers at its widest, plunging to depths of 706 meters, making it Africa's third-deepest freshwater lake. Its extensive shorelines feature rocky cliffs, sandy beaches, dense forests, and wetlands teeming with birdlife. Not-to-miss birding locations include Likoma Island, ideal for spotting Crimson-Rumped Waxbills, Pied kingfishers, and when lucky even the African Pitta, among historical sites and welcoming communities; Chizumulu Island, renowned for tranquil birding environments; the Maleri Island Archipelago, prime birdwatching territory for African Fish Eagles and vibrant cichlid habitats; and the Cape Maclear Peninsula’s Mumbo and Domwe Islands, offering rustic charm, rich birdlife, and picturesque accommodations for wildlife enthusiasts.

Birding at Lake Malawi National Park

Lake Malawi National Park

A Biodiversity Hotspot Located near Cape Maclear and Monkey Bay, Lake Malawi National Park is acclaimed for its extraordinary biodiversity. The park, akin to Africa’s version of the Galápagos, conserves hundreds of endemic cichlid species, attracting global attention from researchers and conservationists. Birdwatchers will find the park irresistible, with frequent sightings of African Fish Eagles, white-breasted cormorants, kingfishers, and Marabou storks.

Why Birdwatchers Love Lake Malawi National Park

Lake Malawi National Park is not only renowned for its aquatic diversity but also hosts diverse terrestrial wildlife, including baboons, hippos, and antelopes. Bird species frequenting this lush landscape include Collared Palm Thrushes, Palm Swifts, Mottled Spinetails, and Southern Brown-throated Weavers.

Popular locations for birding at Lake Malawi

Likoma Island

Likoma Island, nestled in the northeastern part of Lake Malawi, is a haven for bird enthusiasts. Despite its modest size of 18 km², the island boasts over 100 bird species. The varied habitats, including rocky shores, sandy bays, and swamps, support a rich avian diversity. Notable sightings include large colonies of white-breasted cormorants, black-shouldered and yellow-billed kites, and four types of kingfishers. The iconic African fish eagle is frequently observed, its distinctive call echoing across the island . The island's unspoiled environment, characterized by baobab and mango trees, provides an idyllic setting for birdwatching.

Chizumulu Island

Chizumulu Island, the smaller counterpart to Likoma, offers a serene birding experience. The island's hilly terrain, dense forests, and cassava plantations create diverse habitats for various bird species. While specific avian studies are limited, the island's natural environment suggests the presence of species such as hornbills, kingfishers, and swallow-tailed bee-eaters . The island's tranquility and minimal tourist influx make it an ideal spot for birders seeking solitude and undisturbed observation opportunities.

Maleri Islands

The Maleri Islands, comprising three main islands—Maleri, Nankoma, and Nakantenga—are part of the Lake Malawi National Park. These islands are renowned for their pristine environments, featuring rocky outcrops, sandy beaches, and clear waters. Birdwatchers can expect to encounter species such as the African fish eagle, white-breasted cormorant, and various kingfishers. The islands' isolation and protected status contribute to the preservation of their rich avian biodiversity, offering birders a unique and immersive experience.

Cape Maclear Peninsula

Cape Maclear, located at the southern tip of Lake Malawi, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site recognized for its exceptional biodiversity. The area encompasses diverse habitats, including montane forests, rocky shores, and sandy beaches, supporting a wide array of bird species. Birders can observe African fish eagles, white-breasted cormorants, kingfishers, hornbills, nightjars, kestrels, and swallow-tailed bee-eaters . The peninsula's accessibility and range of eco-friendly accommodations make it a popular destination for birdwatching enthusiasts.

Shire River

The Shire River, Malawi's only outlet from Lake Malawi, meanders southward through varied landscapes, including the renowned Liwonde National Park. This riverine environment is a magnet for birdlife, particularly waterbirds and raptors. Birders can expect to encounter species such as the African skimmer (Rynchops flavirostris), known for its unique feeding technique of skimming the water surface with its elongated lower mandible . Other notable species include the saddle-billed stork (Ephippiorhynchus senegalensis), spur-winged lapwing (Vanellus spinosus), and the elusive Pel's fishing owl (Scotopelia peli), which prefers the overhanging riverine forests . The river's diverse habitats, from open waters to reedbeds, support a rich array of bird species, making it a must-visit for birdwatchers.

Nkhata Bay

Situated on the northern shores of Lake Malawi, Nkhata Bay offers a unique blend of aquatic and terrestrial birding opportunities. The area's rocky coastlines, sandy beaches, and adjacent woodlands create diverse habitats that attract various bird species. Birders can observe the African fish eagle (Haliaeetus vocifer), whose distinctive call is emblematic of the region . Other species include the pied kingfisher (Ceryle rudis), known for its hovering hunting technique, and the collared palm thrush (Cichladusa arquata), often found in the area's palm-rich environments. The combination of lake vistas and rich birdlife makes Nkhata Bay a rewarding destination for bird enthusiasts.

Cichlids and Birding Lake Malawi

Lake Malawi’s Global Ecological Treasure Lake Malawi stands out globally for its impressive fish diversity, home to up to 1,000 unique species, predominantly the colorful endemic cichlids. This aquatic abundance significantly enhances the lake's ecological and economic value, supporting local livelihoods and eco-tourism.
