by Eugene Booysen

Liwonde National Park is one of Malawi’s top safari destinations, offering a mix of big game safaris, boat excursions, and birdwatching opportunities. Located in southern Malawi, the park is home to elephants, lions, cheetahs, black rhinos, and over 400 bird species. Visitors can enjoy guided game drives, river safaris on the Shire River, and walking safaris, making it a must-visit destination for wildlife enthusiasts. Birding in Liwonde National Park Paradise

Introduction to Liwonde National Park

Liwonde National Park is one of Malawi’s top safari destinations, offering incredible wildlife encounters, stunning landscapes, and unique conservation success stories. Located in southern Malawi, this park is famous for its boat safaris on the Shire River, impressive elephant populations, and thriving birdlife.

Where is Liwonde National Park?

Liwonde National Park sits in southern Malawi, near the Mozambique border, just 160 km from Blantyre and 250 km from Lilongwe. The Shire River flows through the park, creating a lush, riverine landscape that attracts a wide variety of animals.

What Makes Liwonde National Park Special?

Liwonde isn’t just another safari destination – it’s a conservation success story. Once overrun with poachers, the park has been transformed under African Parks’ management since 2015. The fencing of the park, reintroductions of lions, cheetahs, and wild dogs, and the largest elephant translocation in Malawi’s history have all helped make Liwonde a thriving wildlife haven.

Wildlife and Birding in Liwonde National Park

Animals Found in Liwonde National Park

Liwonde is home to over 17,800 large mammals, making it one of the most diverse wildlife parks in Malawi. The park boasts one of the largest elephant populations in the country, with herds often seen along the riverbanks. Black rhinos have successfully been reintroduced, contributing to a growing breeding population. The park is also home to big cats, including cheetahs, lions, and leopards, which have made a strong comeback thanks to conservation efforts. Large herbivores such as buffalo, hippos, and antelope—including sable antelope, kudu, and eland—thrive in this protected environment. Additionally, crocodiles can frequently be spotted basking on the riverbanks, making Liwonde a prime location for wildlife.

Birds in Liwonde National Park

With over 400 bird species, Liwonde is a top birding destination in Africa. The park is home to an impressive variety of birdlife, including the elusive Pel’s fishing-owl, the colorful Böhm's bee-eater, and the rare Lilian’s lovebird, which is only found in Liwonde within Malawi. Additionally, birdwatchers can spot the striking Livingstone’s flycatcher and various species of vultures, some of which are critically endangered. These rich avian populations make Liwonde a paradise for both casual birdwatchers and dedicated ornithologists alike.

Liwonde National Park Activities

Game Drives and Wildlife Safaris

Liwonde offers some of the best big-game viewing in Malawi. Guided game drives in the morning and evening give visitors the chance to see lions, elephants, and other incredible wildlife up close.

Boat Safaris on the Shire River

A boat safari on the Shire River is one of the highlights of visiting Liwonde. Drifting through hippo pods and crocodile-infested waters, with elephants and buffalo drinking on the banks, is an unforgettable experience.

Walking Safaris: A Closer Look at Nature

Guided walking safaris allow visitors to explore the park on foot, learning about the smaller creatures, plants, and tracking techniques. This is an excellent way to experience the bush up close.

Best Time for Birding in Liwonde National Park

Dry Season vs. Wet Season: What to Expect

  • Dry Season (April – October): Best time for game viewing, as animals gather near water sources.
  • Wet Season (November – March): The park transforms into a lush green paradise, great for birdwatching but harder for spotting big game.

Conservation and Community Involvement

Wildlife Conservation Efforts

African Parks’ management has made Liwonde a poaching-free zone, ensuring the protection of its diverse wildlife. Significant conservation efforts have led to the successful reintroduction of lions, cheetahs, wild dogs, and rhinos, bringing back key predators and strengthening the park’s ecosystem. Additionally, the elephant translocation project has moved over 500 elephants to safer habitats, helping to balance populations and reduce human-wildlife conflict.

Local Communities and Sustainable Tourism

Revenue-sharing programs help local villages benefit from tourism, ensuring that the economic impact of conservation efforts extends beyond the park. Projects like Makanga Women’s Group and Njobvu Cultural Village Lodge play a crucial role in supporting community-driven tourism, creating sustainable livelihoods while preserving the natural beauty and wildlife of Liwonde.
